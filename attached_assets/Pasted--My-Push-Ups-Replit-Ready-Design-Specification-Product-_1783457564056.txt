# My Push Ups — Replit-Ready Design Specification

## Product concept

My Push Ups is an iOS training app focused on one clear job: help users build a push-up habit and progress safely toward higher rep goals through a guided daily habit track and a structured 5-round strength track.[cite:1]

The product should feel simple during use, even though the training logic underneath includes onboarding, level selection, max-rep calibration, 5-round sessions, rest timing, effort-based progression, deload behavior, and periodic re-tests.[cite:1]

## Product principles

### Core UX rules

- One dominant action per screen.
- The active workout screen must center the next required action, not stats or navigation clutter.
- The app should always answer: what should I do now, how much should I do, and what happens next.[cite:1]
- The strength session pattern should remain consistent: show reps, complete reps, start rest, begin next round, finish session, propose next plan.[cite:1]
- Use progressive disclosure: training science stays in the background, plain-language guidance stays in the foreground.

### Emotional tone

The tone should feel calm, athletic, and disciplined rather than aggressive or hyper-gamified. The user should feel coached, not shouted at.

Use a visual language that suggests rhythm and progress: strong typography, restrained color, clear timing states, and subtle completion feedback. Avoid neon fitness clichés, cluttered dashboards, and motivational overload.

## Information architecture

Use a 4-tab structure for v1:

| Tab | Purpose | UX role |
|---|---|---|
| Today | Next recommended workout | Primary daily entry point |
| Plan | Weekly schedule and path to goal | Orientation and planning |
| Progress | Streaks, max tests, completed sessions, milestones | Motivation and review |
| Settings | Rest interval, reminders, sound, haptics, accessibility, training options | Personalization |

Today should always open first because the training plan already determines the next most useful action for the user.[cite:1]

## Primary user flow

### Onboarding

The onboarding flow should be short and trust-building, because the app needs just enough information to generate a safe and personalized plan.[cite:1]

Recommended onboarding sequence:

1. Welcome screen: short value proposition.
2. Goal selection: 20, 30, 50, 100, or custom.
3. Basic health screening: cardiovascular issues, major joint or spine problems, current chest/shoulder/wrist pain.[cite:1]
4. Starting level selection: wall, incline, knee, or full push-ups.[cite:1]
5. Guided max test at the selected level, stopping at technical limit rather than absolute failure.[cite:1]
6. Plan preview: show today’s session and the weekly structure.[cite:1]
7. Immediate CTA: Start Day 1.

If the user flags a serious health issue, the app must show a clear safety warning and require acknowledgment before continuing.[cite:1]

### Daily entry

The Today screen should show a single recommended card at the top:

- Quick habit session, or
- Strength session, depending on the day in the plan.[cite:1]

Secondary content may include the weekly streak, tomorrow preview, and the current goal progress, but these should remain visually subordinate to the primary start action.

### Active workout

The active workout screen is the heart of the product. It should use a single central object that changes state instead of switching between unrelated layouts.

#### Work state

Show:

- Round label above the circle, for example “Round 2 of 5.”[cite:1]
- Large rep number inside a circular control.[cite:1]
- Short helper text below, such as “Tap when done.”
- Secondary controls below the fold line: pause, finish early, sound toggle.

Interaction:

- User performs the displayed reps.
- User taps the big circle to confirm completion.[cite:1]
- Completion triggers haptic feedback and transitions into rest mode.

#### Rest state

Show the same circle transformed into a countdown ring.

Display:

- Remaining time in the center.
- Label above: “Rest.”
- Small secondary action: Next round now.
- Optional pause action.

Behavior:

- Default rest is 120 seconds for the strength track, adjustable in settings.[cite:1]
- The user can proceed early by tapping to start the next round.[cite:1]
- When the timer ends, the next round starts automatically and the rep target appears again.[cite:1]

#### Session complete state

At session completion, show:

- Large success message.
- Total rounds completed.
- Total reps completed.
- Optional perceived effort input using a simple 1–10 scale, because progression logic depends on completion and effort.[cite:1]
- A next-step card: “Tomorrow: quick habit set” or “Next strength day: 5 rounds of 8.”[cite:1]

Use a celebratory microinteraction, such as a subtle pulse burst or checkmark ripple, rather than loud confetti.

## Screen-by-screen specification

### 1. Welcome screen

Purpose: establish trust and clarity.

Content:

- App name: My Push Ups.
- Tagline: Build your push-up habit. Progress to 100.
- Short explanation: personalized plan based on current ability.
- CTA: Get started.
- Secondary link: How it works.

Layout:

- Top-safe-area breathing room.
- Centered hero text.
- One visual anchor, such as a circular progress motif.
- Bottom-anchored CTA for thumb reach.

### 2. Health check screen

Purpose: capture required safety inputs before plan generation.[cite:1]

Content:

- Three yes/no health questions.
- Short explanatory note.
- Continue CTA.
- If needed, warning sheet with acknowledgment button.[cite:1]

Design note: use plain language and avoid medical-dashboard styling.

### 3. Level selection screen

Purpose: place the user into the correct push-up variation.[cite:1]

Cards:

- Wall push-ups
- Incline push-ups
- Knee push-ups
- Full push-ups

Each card should include:

- Name
- Short description
- Simple line illustration or icon
- “Best for…” helper text

Selection behavior should feel guided, not quiz-like.

### 4. Max test screen

Purpose: establish the user’s current baseline.[cite:1]

Elements:

- Instruction card: stop when form breaks.
- Form cues: neutral spine, elbows around 45 degrees, controlled range of motion.[cite:1]
- Start test CTA.
- Rep counter input after completion.
- Confirm result CTA.

Design note: reassure the user that this is not an all-out competition test; it is calibration.[cite:1]

### 5. Today screen

Purpose: launch the next correct action fast.

Structure:

- Greeting or date header.
- Main workout card.
- Small weekly schedule strip.
- Progress toward current goal.
- Optional previous result summary.

Primary card examples:

- “Today: Quick habit set — 1 round of 6.”[cite:1]
- “Today: Strength session — 5 rounds of 8, rest 2:00.”[cite:1]

### 6. Workout screen

Purpose: one-screen training execution.

Layout hierarchy:

1. Top: session type and round count.
2. Center: big circular target.
3. Bottom: supporting controls.

Circle states:

- Work: solid filled circle with rep number.
- Rest: stroked progress ring with countdown.
- Complete: confirmation state with check indicator.

### 7. Plan screen

Purpose: explain the structure without making the user interpret sports science.

Sections:

- Current level
- Weekly schedule
- Current session prescription
- Next re-test timing
- Goal path to 100

Use plain-language cards instead of complex charts, for example:

- “You completed 2 strong sessions in a row. Next workout adds 1 rep.”[cite:1]
- “Re-test due this week.”[cite:1]

### 8. Progress screen

Purpose: reinforce momentum.

Modules:

- Weekly streak
- Total sessions completed
- Best max test
- Current level
- Milestones unlocked
- Last 4 weeks summary

Good motivation features for this app:

- Streak ring
- Milestone ladder: 10, 20, 30, 50, 75, 100
- Small personal records
- “You are stronger than 2 weeks ago” style summaries based on actual training data

### 9. Settings screen

Purpose: let users tune the experience without weakening the core flow.

Include:

- Rest interval, default 120s, adjustable within plan-supported range.[cite:1]
- Sound on/off
- Haptics on/off
- Reminders
- Goal editing
- Accessibility options
- Motion reduction
- Training help and form guidance

Keep settings in grouped lists with short descriptions.

## Component system

Build the prototype around reusable components.

### BigCircleCard

Purpose: main workout control.

States:

- Idle-work
- Pressed-work
- Resting-countdown
- Paused-rest
- Completed-round

Specs:

- Diameter mobile: 240–280 pt
- Shape: perfect circle
- Content: large numeral centered, helper label below or above
- Motion: soft scale on tap, ring animation during rest

### WorkoutHeader

Fields:

- Session type
- Round progress
- Level label
- Optional close button

### RestTimerRing

Fields:

- Remaining time
- Total rest duration
- Skip now CTA
- Pause/resume action

### PlanDayCard

Fields:

- Day label
- Session type
- Reps x rounds
- Status: done / due / upcoming

### MilestoneBadge

Use for:

- 10 reps
- 20 reps
- 7-day streak
- First max re-test
- Level upgrade

### SettingRow

Patterns:

- Navigation row
- Toggle row
- Stepper row
- Inline picker row

## Interaction design best practices

### Make one object do the work

The big circle should remain the anchor throughout the session. During work it shows reps; during rest it becomes the timer; after the timer it returns to reps.[cite:1]

This object continuity reduces cognitive load and creates an intuitive workout rhythm.

### Keep metrics out of the way

Do not overload the active session with:

- weekly graphs
- calories
- badges
- motivational quotes
- community content

These belong before or after the workout, not during it.

### Use microfeedback, not noise

Recommended:

- light haptic on round completion
- stronger haptic when rest ends
- subtle pulse when the next round appears
- quiet completion animation

Avoid:

- flashing backgrounds
- excessive sound effects
- large confetti explosions
- animated mascots

### Make early skip safe and obvious

The user should be able to start the next round early during rest, because your training method allows proceeding early by tapping next round.[cite:1]

That action should be visible but secondary so the user does not accidentally skip recovery.

### Design for physical context

Users may be breathing hard, moving, or on the floor. Therefore:

- Keep the primary control large.
- Keep text large and sparse.
- Avoid precise gestures.
- Place the main action in the lower-middle visual field.
- Ensure major controls meet 44x44 pt minimum touch size.

## Visual design system

### Art direction

Use a calm athletic product aesthetic: precise, strong, and premium without looking clinical.

This app should look more like a focused training instrument than a general-purpose fitness marketplace.

### Color palette

Recommended primary palette:

- Background light: warm off-white, #F6F4EF
- Surface light: #FFFFFF
- Text dark: #171717
- Muted text: #6B6B6B
- Accent primary: performance coral-red, #E44F3A
- Accent dark state: #C63E2B
- Success: #2E8B57
- Warning: #B86A1E
- Background dark: #111111
- Surface dark: #1C1C1C
- Text light: #F4F4F4

Why this works:

- Warm neutrals make the app feel less sterile.
- Coral-red signals action and effort better than generic blue.
- The palette remains clean enough for daily use.

### Typography

Recommended pairing for prototype:

- Display / large numerals: General Sans or Satoshi Bold
- UI text: Inter or General Sans Regular/Medium

Type scale:

- Hero numeral inside circle: 72–96 pt
- Screen title: 28–32 pt
- Section title: 20–24 pt
- Body: 16–17 pt
- Small metadata: 13–14 pt

Rules:

- Numerals are the visual hero.
- Avoid decorative display fonts.
- Maintain high contrast and generous line spacing.

### Spacing and shape

- Base spacing unit: 4 pt
- Common spacing rhythm: 8, 12, 16, 24, 32
- Card radius: 20–24 pt
- Button radius: 16–20 pt
- Circle: fully round
- Borders: subtle, low-contrast, 1 pt

### Motion

Motion should communicate state change, not decoration.

Use:

- Circle scale-down then settle on tap
- Circular ring countdown during rest
- Number fade/scale transition between rounds
- Card slide-up on completion

Respect reduced-motion settings by swapping animated transitions for fade-only states.

## iOS-specific recommendations

- Use SF Symbols where small utility icons are needed.
- Respect safe areas, especially on the workout screen.
- Use haptics as part of the workout rhythm.
- Support Dynamic Type for all non-circle text.
- Keep the workout screen readable in bright and dark conditions.
- Provide Live Activity later as a future enhancement for rest countdowns.

## UX copy style

Use short, direct, supportive copy.

Examples:

- “Round 3 of 5”
- “Do 8 push-ups”
- “Tap when done”
- “Rest for 2:00”
- “Start next round”
- “Workout complete”
- “Tomorrow: quick habit set”
- “You’re ready to add 1 rep next time”

Avoid:

- aggressive slogans
- long onboarding explanations
- sports-science jargon on primary screens
- guilt-driven reminders

## Empty, loading, and error states

### Empty states

Examples:

- No progress yet: “Your training history starts with the first session.”
- No reminders set: “Add a reminder to build your routine.”

Each empty state should include one clear action.

### Loading states

Use skeleton cards for:

- Today workout card
- Plan screen
- Progress metrics

Do not skeletonize the workout circle in a dramatic way; keep loading understated.

### Errors

If something fails, keep the message plain:

- “Couldn’t load today’s workout.”
- “Try again.”

Never show raw technical errors.

## Accessibility requirements

- All interactive controls must be reachable with VoiceOver.
- The big circle must have explicit accessibility labels, for example “Complete round, 8 push-ups.”
- Timer announcements should be meaningful but not noisy.
- Color alone must never signal state.
- Ensure text contrast meets AA standards.
- Provide a high-contrast mode variant if possible.
- Respect reduced motion and larger text preferences.

## Design anti-patterns to avoid

- Gamified clutter on the active workout screen.
- Tiny timer controls.
- A dashboard-first home screen.
- Too many tabs.
- Blue-purple neon fitness aesthetics.
- Generic stock-athlete imagery.
- Text inside the App Store icon.
- Over-complicated onboarding with too many questions up front.
- Forcing users to understand progression rules manually.

## App Store icon specification

### Icon strategy

The icon should be symbolic, bold, and instantly legible at small size. A literal human figure doing a push-up is too detailed for App Store sizes.

### Recommended concept

Use a circular badge with a white abstract push-up mark on a coral-red background.

Composition:

- Background: solid coral-red
- Foreground: simplified white symbol
- Symbol idea: one curved line and two anchor points suggesting body position and downward movement
- Optional subtle ring reference around the mark to echo rep progress and rest timing

### Icon style rules

- No text in the icon
- No tiny stopwatch, dumbbell, or counter details
- No photorealism
- No glossy skeuomorphism
- No multi-object collage
- Keep the silhouette readable on a dark and light App Store background

### Alternative icon directions

1. Abstract downward chevron inside a ring, evoking both a push-up descent and repetition.
2. Monogram-style “PU” fused into a minimal training mark.
3. A single geometric line figure using two dots and one strong stroke.

### Icon color recommendations

Primary version:

- Background: #E44F3A
- Symbol: #FFFFFF

Alternate testing versions:

- Deep navy background with white symbol
- Warm black background with coral symbol

### App Store icon evaluation checklist

- Recognizable at 60x60 px
- Distinct from generic fitness icons
- Reads as strength and repetition, not cardio
- Works without text
- Survives rounded mask cropping

## Final design direction summary

My Push Ups should feel like a focused coach in your pocket: clear, rhythmic, and quietly motivating. The main experience should revolve around one central circle, one next action, and one trustworthy plan shaped by the user’s actual ability and progression history.[cite:1]

The Replit prototype should therefore prioritize workflow clarity, state transitions, strong numeric hierarchy, and a reusable component system over visual ornament or feature sprawl. The product becomes memorable not by adding more UI, but by making the training loop feel effortless and repeatable.[cite:1]
